Neighbors

on the homunculus, the womb, and why you noticed

My mother-in-love once told me that in her culture, the body was divided differently. Women were often bare-breasted. Not to prove anything. Not to be bold. Just because breasts fed children. They were functional. They were there. But the wrappers went all the way down to the ankles. Always. She told me the feet were the entrance to the womb.

I laughed when she said it. Not because I thought she was wrong, but because it sounded so specific. Of all the things to guard, that is what you chose? Still, it stayed with me. I tried walking in one of those wraps once, and you cannot move the same way. Your steps get shorter. You kind of shuffle. Your body adjusts without you trying. I remember wondering if that is how they balanced things on their head, because the way you move changes when something is held differently. So when she talked about girls being scandalous in knee-length skirts, I laughed again. Where I am from, that would not even register. But the entrance to the womb part did not leave.

Not as the real truth. As a way of thinking. It made me realize the body is not fixed in meaning. What is protected, what is exposed, and what is neutral — those are choices. In one place, breasts mean very little. In another, they carry everything. In one place, feet are just feet. In another, they are about movement. They carry you toward something. That alone can make them worth covering.

What is hidden gets attention. What is controlled becomes important. What is left alone fades into the background.

So if you notice something specific — something you feel like you are not supposed to notice — it does not mean something is wrong with you. It often means your attention had somewhere to land, and it stayed there. And maybe you never said it out loud.

·

At first, I thought it came from how the body develops — how things start close together and then spread out — but that is only part of the story.

There is also a brain piece to this, and it is easier to understand than it sounds. The brain isn't made of locked rooms. It's more like a hallway with doors close together. Most of the time, each door stays separate. What you feel in one place stays in that place.

But some people's brains have doors that are really, really close.

And when the doors are that close, things don't always stay separate. Signals move. They overlap. They pass through each other more easily. That does not mean something is broken. It just means the boundary is a little more open.

In the brain's sensory map, the areas for the feet and the genitals sit next to each other. This was first mapped by neurosurgeon Wilder Penfield, who called his distorted little figure the homunculus — a man drawn onto the brain, body parts sized by how much cortical real estate they occupy. V.S. Ramachandran later explored how signals can cross between these neighboring regions. The foot and what lies above it have always been close. The brain confirms what the wrap already knew.

This is one reason feet show up more often than elbows or shoulders. Culture still matters. What you see, what is hidden, and what is talked about all shape attention. But the brain gives it somewhere to connect.

·

There is another layer to this. People are naturally curious about what is hidden. When something is covered or boxed up, it pulls attention. In the West, for a long time, women covered everything below the waist with what was essentially a tent — a dress. And when that skirt got lifted, even a little, people looked. They also hid that they were looking.

We have stories about this. Pandora's box is one of them. When something is closed off, we want to open it. When something is hidden, we want to see it.

That is why it is not just about feet.

In some cultures, a woman's face is covered, and the eyes become the most striking thing. In others, the legs are covered, and anything near them starts to carry more weight. It shifts depending on what is allowed and what is not. So it is not surprising that things like socks or pantyhose — things that partially hide and partially show — become meaningful too.

The feet, though, are a little different. Because they sit in that same space culturally — sometimes hidden, sometimes seen — but they also sit right next door in the brain to something more private.

So they carry both.

Once attention settles, it tends to stay. Over time, that can feel very personal. It can feel like something that defines you. But it is not random. It is patterned.

·

You didn't make this up.

This does not mean something is wrong with you. It means you noticed something, and it stayed. There are reasons for that. What you saw. What you didn't see. How your brain connects things. None of that makes you broken.

Some people move on quickly. Some people don't. If your attention stays, it stays. That's not the problem.

The problem is what you do with it.

You don't have to pretend this isn't there. You don't have to feel guilty for noticing something. Whatever your thing is — whatever you're drawn to, whatever you keep coming back to — it has a story. It didn't come from nowhere. That story matters. But not as much as how you act on it.

No force. No fear. You don't push it on people. You don't hide it like it's something dirty either. You learn how to carry it. That means being honest without pressure. Clear without making someone uncomfortable. Aware that another person is involved.

You can want what you want. You just don't get to ignore how you carry it.

·

Instead of asking, what's wrong with me — ask, where did this come from?

Because it didn't come from nowhere. And it doesn't make you less of a man. It just means you noticed something, and you stayed with it.

Let it come up the right way. Not forced. Not hidden.

Just handled well.
